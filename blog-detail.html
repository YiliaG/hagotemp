Helping to Manage and Cure Diabetes.

News 24 / 04 / 2017

Fifty50 was started in 1989 by 3 people with 3 products made especially for individuals with diabetes. One had diabetes, one was a diabetes educator, and one was a volunteer raising money for diabetes research. Our dream was to create great tasting Low Glycemic foods that individuals living with diabetes could enjoy while managing their disease and, at the same time, try to help find a diabetes cure.

Today, Fifty50 Foods is a leader in helping individuals manage diabetes and funding research to find a diabetes cure. Our product line now includes 23 Low Glycemic foods designed for a Low Glycemic diet that minimizes blood glucose fluctuations. Whether you are living with diabetes, have chronic kidney disease or are an athlete, we have products that will help control you blood glucose levels.

More importantly, Fifty50 Foods donates 50% of profits to diabetes research. In 1991 we made our first contribution of $15,620 and as the company has grown, so have our contributions, to date, we have contributed over $13.6 million to diabetes research. We are confident our efforts and those of so many others will lead to a diabetes cure.

he glycemic index (GI) is a ranking of carbohydrates on a scale from 0 to 100 according to the extent to which they raise blood sugar levels after eating. Foods with a high GI are those which are rapidly digested and absorbed and result in marked fluctuations in blood sugar levels. Low-GI foods, by virtue of their slow digestion and absorption, produce gradual rises in blood sugar and insulin levels, and have proven benefits for health. Low GI diets have been shown to improve both glucose and lipid levels in people with diabetes (type 1 and type 2). They have benefits for weight control because they help control appetite and delay hunger. Low GI diets also reduce insulin levels and insulin resistance.

Recent studies from Harvard School of Public Health indicate that the risks of diseases such as type 2 diabetes and coronary heart disease are strongly related to the GI of the overall diet. In 1999, the World Health Organisation (WHO) and Food and Agriculture Organisation (FAO) recommended that people in industrialised countries base their diets on low-GI foods in order to prevent the most common diseases of affluence, such as coronary heart disease, diabetes and obesity.
